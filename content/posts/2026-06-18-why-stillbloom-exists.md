There's a moment every flower-lover knows too well. You buy a bunch — peonies, perhaps, or a loose spray of ranunculus — carry them home with quiet ceremony, arrange them in your favourite vase, and for five days they're the most beautiful thing in the room.

Then they're not.

We started Stillbloom with a simple question: *why must beauty be so contingent?* Why should the flowers at your wedding altar look pristine for the ceremony and tired by the dinner? Why should a gift that costs thought and care brown at the edges within a week?

The answer, it turns out, is that it doesn't have to.

## The Old Art of Stillness

Drying flowers is one of the oldest forms of preservation. It was practised in ancient Egypt, treasured in Victorian England, and carried across cultures as a way of holding onto something irreplaceable. The craft never disappeared — it just waited quietly, which is very much in its nature.

What's changed is how we do it, and why.

Today's dried botanicals are nothing like the crumbling lavender bundles of childhood memory. Harvested at the precise peak of bloom, slow-dried over weeks without heat or chemicals, they retain an extraordinary fidelity to the living flower — the curve of the stem, the texture of the petal, the quiet dignity of something that once grew in soil under sky.

## What We Believe

Stillbloom exists because we believe the most beautiful things in your home should outlast the week. That a bouquet should be a decision, not a subscription. That the flowers at your wedding deserve to be on your shelf on your tenth anniversary.

We source from small farms — mostly in Europe, all committed to practices that don't extract more than they give back. We dry slowly, compose by hand, and wrap with the care of something that's meant to matter.

Nothing is rushed. Everything is considered.
